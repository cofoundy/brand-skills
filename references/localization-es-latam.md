# Localization — Spanish / LATAM

Brand Skills is built bilingual. When the brand, audience, or market is Spanish-speaking (especially Latin
America), every skill should apply the rules below alongside its core framework. The `naming` skill
in particular must screen candidates against Spanish phonetics and regional connotation, not just
English.

## When to apply

Apply LATAM rules when ANY of:
- The product's primary market is a Spanish-speaking country.
- The audience persona's first language is Spanish.
- The brand will operate under Peruvian / LATAM trademark and consumer-law regimes.

If the brand is global, screen names in **both** English and Spanish — a name that's clean in
English can be unusable in Spanish (and vice versa).

## Naming — Spanish phonosemantics & screening

1. **Pronounceability.** A name must be pronounceable by a Spanish speaker on first read. Spanish is
   phonetic — letters map to fixed sounds. Watch for:
   - `j` → harsh /x/ ("jota"), not English /dʒ/. "Jolt" reads wrong.
   - `h` is silent. "Hatch" loses its first sound.
   - `v`/`b` are the same sound (/b/). "Vibe"/"Bibe" collapse.
   - `ll` → /ʝ/, `ñ` exists, `z`/`c` → /s/ in LATAM (seseo).
   - English vowel digraphs (ea, ou, igh) have no Spanish reading → spelling-by-ear fails.
2. **Connotation screen (regional, not just dictionary).** Check the candidate against slang in
   major markets — **Mexico, Colombia, Argentina, Peru, Chile** at minimum. Words innocuous in one
   country are vulgar or comic in another. Flag any hit; do not ship a name with an unscreened
   double meaning.
3. **Accent & keyboard.** Prefer names typable without accents/`ñ` for domains/handles/CLI, OR
   commit to the accent everywhere. Never let the canonical spelling drift between accented and
   unaccented.
4. **Gender & article.** Spanish nouns carry gender; a coined name will attract "el"/"la". Say it
   with both articles aloud — pick one that doesn't sound awkward or unintentionally gendered.
5. **Diminutives.** Spanish speakers naturally diminutize ("-ito/-ita"). Test the diminutive — if it
   becomes silly or rude, reconsider.

## Voice, messaging, copy

- **Tuteo vs ustedeo.** Default to **tuteo** ("tú") for startup/consumer brands in most of LATAM;
  use **usted** for formal/enterprise/legal or markets that expect it (parts of Colombia, formal
  Peru). Set this in `brand-voice` and keep it consistent.
- **No "neutral Spanish" cop-out for local brands.** "Español neutro" reads as foreign/translated.
  For a single-market brand, write in that market's register.
- **Date & number format.** `DD/MM/YYYY`, decimal comma (`S/ 1.299,90`), 24h time. Never US format
  in LATAM-facing copy.
- **Inclusive language.** Follow the brand's stance explicitly; don't default to "-x/-e" forms
  without a decision recorded in `brand-voice`.

## Trademark & legal (pointer)

Public availability checks (whois/npm/GitHub) are necessary but **not** a trademark clearance. For
LATAM commercial use, a name must also clear the national IP registry (e.g. **INDECOPI** in Peru).
Brand Skills does not file marks; treat registry clearance as a separate step before committing a name to
market.
